Biblical Scholarship and the Book of Mormon

This section provides an in-depth exploration of what biblical scholarship can reveal about the Book of Mormon’s claims as an ancient, divinely preserved text.

Adam and Eve in the Book of Mormon

We examine the historicity of Adam and Eve and its implications for the Book of Mormon, the Book of Abraham, the Doctrine and Covenants, and the temple ceremony. Additionally, we explore how Joseph Smith identified Adam as the "Ancient of Days," tracing the origins of this idea.

The Global Flood and the Book of Mormon

This section explores the historicity of a global flood and its impact on the Book of Mormon, the Book of Abraham, and the Book of Moses. We also review the apologetic argument that belief in a literal flood is not necessary for church membership, comparing this stance with scriptural references to a flood.

The Tower of Babel and the Book of Mormon

Here, we explore the historicity of the Tower of Babel and its implications for the Book of Mormon, especially regarding the Jaredites, who are said to have migrated to the Americas following the confounding of language

The King James Bible and the Book of Mormon

This section examines the Book of Mormon's reliance on the King James Bible, analyzing what this connection suggests about the book’s authenticity and claims of ancient origin. We investigate mistranslations, later additions, and the use of Jesus’ Sermon on the Mount to assess whether their inclusion in the Book of Mormon is historically plausible and what it reveals about Joseph Smith’s translation process.

The Sermon on the Mount in the Book of Mormon

We take a closer look at the inclusion of the King James Version’s Sermon on the Mount in 3 Nephi, where it appears as the Sermon at the Temple. This section also discusses the historicity of the Sermon on the Mount, its appearance in the Synoptic Gospels, and the role of the hypothetical Q source in shaping the accounts in Matthew and Luke.

The Long Ending of Mark and the Book of Mormon

We review the scholarly consensus on the "Long Ending of Mark," which was not part of the original Gospel manuscripts, and examine how this late addition appears, word-for-word, in the Book of Mormon (Mormon 9:24) through a copy from the King James Bible.

Deutero-Isaiah and the Book of Mormon

This section addresses the challenge posed by "Deutero-Isaiah," as modern scholarship dates Isaiah chapters 40-55 to after 586 BCE, while the brass plates in the Book of Mormon were purportedly taken from Laban in 600 BCE. We analyze the extensive use of this material in the Book of Mormon and the difficulties it presents for its claims of ancient authenticity.
