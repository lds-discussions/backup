Overview of Polygamy, Part 1: Timeline and Introduction

In my previous overviews, I primarily focused on scriptural discussions with a few insights into the church’s foundational events. So far, I've written sixteen overviews on the Book of Mormon and biblical scholarship, two sections on the Book of Abraham, and two covering historical issues on the First Vision and the restoration of the priesthood.

Now, it’s time to address polygamy—a distinct and complex topic compared to the others. I want to highlight the historical issues surrounding it and share that, personally, this was the first issue in church history that deeply troubled me as both a convert and a believing member. While I remain a member of record, my concerns with polygamy contributed significantly to my current inactivity; it has always felt unresolved and problematic.

This issue unsettles me on an emotional level, beyond the historical problems of previous overviews. I share this upfront because it's uncomfortable, and reading about how Joseph Smith practiced polygamy brings up feelings of anger, disgust, and embarrassment.

As a convert, I was never informed that Joseph Smith himself practiced polygamy. While I was aware polygamy was part of church history and doctrine, I didn’t know the specifics of its implementation. Given the weight of this topic in understanding church history, I've divided the material into three parts to keep it manageable. Hopefully, this structure will make it more accessible for anyone working through the details.

If you're unfamiliar with the church's history of polygamy, I want to prepare you: these overviews might be uncomfortable. I recently came across a tweet that aptly captured this sentiment:

“Studying history will sometimes disturb you.
Studying history will sometimes upset you.
Studying history will sometimes make you furious.
If studying history always makes you feel proud and happy, you probably aren’t studying history.”

I'm making this clear because these sections are markedly different from prior overviews. The topic of polygamy brings up a lot of difficult material, including accounts of Joseph Smith's relationships with younger women, his use of authority to persuade them, and the secrecy involved, often without his first wife Emma’s knowledge.

What I've observed about polygamy is that many members find ways to rationalize it, often by not scrutinizing how it actually happened. It's one of those areas where the church tends to say, "It’ll all work out" or "We just don’t know." Yet, this is still a doctrine of the church. I believe every member should understand how Joseph Smith practiced and introduced polygamy, as it remains church doctrine.

For women in the church, this doctrine has significant implications for the future. While many say "we always have a choice," in polygamy, that choice is often framed as either acceptance or "destruction." This is not an exaggeration, as I’ll explain in detail. Although the church may not discuss it openly, this doctrine remains foundational in the concept of eternal life for every woman.

While my previous twenty-two overviews have shown that the church may not align with its truth claims, this series will illustrate that some of Joseph Smith’s and the church’s unique doctrines may not align with concepts of goodness either. I mention this upfront because, for a believing member, these sections may be challenging—but they are necessary for a complete understanding of church history and the evidence against its truth claims.

From the church’s most recent handbook changes:

In today’s world, information is easy to access and share. This can be a great blessing for those seeking to be educated and informed. However, many sources of information are unreliable and do not edify. Some sources seek to promote anger, contention, fear, or baseless conspiracy theories (see 3 Nephi 11:30; Mosiah 2:32). Therefore, it is important that Church members be wise as they seek truth.” (Official Church Handbook)

To be clear: The true history of polygamy is in no way edifying. The church advises against sources that don't edify, but these topics, like the others, are based on documented, evidence-based history. If they don't uplift, it isn’t due to the influence of Satan or the ‘adversary’; it's because polygamy's history is far more troubling than what members are typically taught.

Before diving into Joseph Smith’s polygamy and its place in the church, I’d like to provide context by examining how other self-proclaimed prophets initiated polygamous or sexual relationships with their followers. As Jonathan Streeter highlighted in his presentation at Sunstone regarding Joseph Smith and the Happiness Letter to Nancy Rigdon:

"Keep in mind that special divine permission is nothing new... Self proclaimed prophet David Koresh and the Branch Davidians claimed special divine permission to take child brides for the purpose of producing the 24 elders foretold in the Book of Revelations. Self proclaimed prophet Wayne Bent of the Lord Our Righteousness Church claimed special divine permission for having sexual relations with children, even his own daughter in law in order to avoid God's punishment. Self proclaimed prophet Julius Shacknow of the sect known as The Work, claimed special privilege to promise salvation in exchange for sexual intercourse with women and children, including his own stepdaughter. Self proclaimed prophet Tony Alamo of Alamo Christian Ministries claimed special biblical permission to illegally marry multiple women and children. Self proclaimed prophet David Berg of the Children of God claimed special divine permission to normalize sexual relation with children. Prophets, justifying their own predations as special, divine permission through the use of pious language and religious sentiment, is nothing new." (Mormon Discussions Podcasts. 342: Nancy Rigdon and the Happiness Letter)

In this first overview, I'll present a timeline and background of polygamy and the creation of Doctrine & Covenants 132. In part two, I’ll explore how Joseph Smith practiced polygamy and polyandry, including examples of those who accepted and those who rejected his advances. Finally, in part three, we’ll examine the apologetic responses to polygamy.

An Overview of the Polygamy Timeline

In our annotated essay on polygamy, we explain why the church’s claim that the revelation was given to Joseph Smith in 1831 is not supported by evidence. Therefore, it’s essential to establish a concise timeline of key events leading up to the creation of Doctrine & Covenants 132:

  • 1833: Joseph Smith begins a polygamous/extramarital relationship with Fanny Alger. The church later describes this as a marriage, but no records from the time exist, and the first mention of marriage appears over 60 years after the event.
  • 1835: The church, under Joseph Smith's leadership, adds a section to the Doctrine and Covenants condemning polygamy.
  • 1836: Joseph Smith reports a vision of Elijah and Elias to restore priesthood keys, later interpreted to include the authority for sealing marriages.
  • 1841: Joseph’s polygamous marriages in Nauvoo begin with Louisa Beaman.
  • 1842: Joseph temporarily suspends polygamous marriages after John Bennett's “spiritual wifery” scandal is exposed.
  • 1843: Joseph resumes polygamy, marrying more than a dozen women, bringing the total to over 30.
  • July 12, 1843: Joseph Smith dictates a revelation on polygamy to William Clayton, which later becomes Doctrine & Covenants 132.

The Book of Mormon and Polygamy

In previous discussions, I’ve presented reasons for viewing Joseph Smith as the author of the Book of Mormon. In those analyses, I highlighted instances where Smith integrates his own experiences and the surrounding ideas of his time. This context makes the Book of Mormon’s section on polygamy, found in Jacob 2, particularly noteworthy.

24 Behold, David and Solomon truly had many wives and concubines, which thing was abominable before me, saith the Lord.

25 Wherefore, thus saith the Lord, I have led this people forth out of the land of Jerusalem, by the power of mine arm, that I might raise up unto me a righteous branch from the fruit of the loins of Joseph.

26 Wherefore, I the Lord God will not suffer that this people shall do like unto them of old.

27 Wherefore, my brethren, hear me, and hearken to the word of the Lord: For there shall not any man among you have save it be one wife; and concubines he shall have none;

28 For I, the Lord God, delight in the chastity of women. And whoredoms are an abomination before me; thus saith the Lord of Hosts.

29 Wherefore, this people shall keep my commandments, saith the Lord of Hosts, or cursed be the land for their sakes.

30 For if I will, saith the Lord of Hosts, raise up seed unto me, I will command my people; otherwise they shall hearken unto these things.
(Book of Mormon. Jacob 2: 24-30)

In the Book of Mormon, God tells the people, “to excuse themselves in committing whoredoms, because of the things which were written concerning David, and Solomon his son” (Jacob 2:23).

Here, the author of the Book of Mormon appears to reference David and Solomon to clarify a moral point for the reader. This text could be interpreted as providing a potential loophole for polygamy, although it's unclear if this was the author's intention.

This passage is especially significant because, as we will see when we examine Doctrine and Covenants 132, Joseph Smith does more than just contradict the Book of Mormon’s stance on polygamy—he reframes how God views David and Solomon’s many wives and concubines.

The 1831 Revelation on Polygamy

The heading for Doctrine and Covenants 132 includes the following:

“Although the revelation was recorded in 1843, evidence indicates that some of the principles involved in this revelation were known by the Prophet as early as 1831.”

The church’s essay on polygamy in Kirtland and Nauvoo also states this idea:

“The revelation on plural marriage was not written down until 1843, but its early verses suggest that part of it emerged from Joseph Smith’s study of the Old Testament in 1831. People who knew Joseph well later stated he received the revelation about that time.”

There is one big reason that the church wants to pin polygamy to 1831 and that is his affair with Fanny Alger. We will cover that more below, but it’s important to note here because the history behind the 1831 revelation is not helpful to the church’s case.

The “people who knew Joseph well” referred to here are W.W. Phelps, Oliver Cowdery and five other men who received instruction for their mission to the ‘Indians’ to take additional wives to make the Indians whiter. From the claimed revelation:

“[I]t is my will, that in time, ye should take unto you wives of the Lamanites and Nephites, that their posterity may become white, delightsome, and Just, for even now their females are more virtuous than the gentiles.” (W.W. Phelps, 1831)

W.W. Phelps was a friendly source, and we also have an antagonistic source that confirms that Joseph Smith claimed a revelation from God that they should marry the Native Americas, who Joseph Smith labeled as Lamanites in claimed revelations from God. From Ezra Booth:

“...it has been made known by revelation, that it will be pleasing to the Lord, should they form a matrimonial alliance with the natives; and by this means the Elders, who comply with the thing so pleasing to the Lord, and for which the Lord has promised to bless those who do it abundantly, gain a residence in the Indian territory, independent of the agent....” (Ohio Star, December 8, 1831)

This revelation was probably not mentioned specifically in the church’s Gospel Topics essay because of its racist overtones and contradiction to the instructions in D&C 132. I want to again point out this line from W.W. Phelps:

“ye should take unto you wives of the Lamanites and Nephites, that their posterity may become white, delightsome, and Just.”

As we discussed in our overview on DNA and the Book of Mormon, the teaching of the Book of Mormon was that the Native Americans were cursed with a dark skin for their wickedness, and if they came back to Christ, their skin would again turn ‘white and delightsome.’ This claimed revelation from God has Joseph Smith teaching this concept again, with the idea that if the more righteous leaders of the church have sex and procreate with the Native Americans, that their children would become whiter.

This is the harm that is caused from an incorrect teaching such as the premise of the Book of Mormon, where Native Americans are given a history and identity that is simply not true. While it is true that children of Native Americans would look whiter if they have a child with a white settler, it is not because the child is “white, delightsome, and just,” but because that child would have DNA from the white father.

The one other reference we have to Joseph Smith speaking about polygamy in 1831 is another one the church does not want to bring up. Mary Elizabeth Rollins Lightner, who is famous in church history for protecting the copies of the Book of Commandments against the mob in Missouri, was later a polyandrous wife of Joseph Smith. She gives this account of her introduction to polygamy:

Mary Elizabeth Rollins Lightner: "[At age 12 in 1831], [Smith] told me about his great vision concerning me. He said I was the first woman God commanded him to take as a plural wife. … In 1834 he was commanded to take me for a Wife …. [In 1842 I] went forward and was sealed to him. Brigham Young performed the sealing … for time, and all Eternity. I did just as Joseph told me to do." (Compton, Todd (1997), In Sacred Loneliness: The Plural Wives of Joseph Smith)

To be clear, I do not believe that Joseph Smith was telling Mary at the age of 12 that he was going to take her for a wife. Many stories in church history are altered and embellished to become faith promoting and, in some cases, self-serving, and there is absolutely no reason to believe Joseph Smith was thinking of polygamy at this point, let alone with a 12 year old girl.

That said, this is another data point that shows Joseph Smith was thinking about polygamy in 1831, but it is not one the church is wanting to reference. While we will see later that Joseph Smith does marry two 14 year old girls, the idea that he is grooming a 12 year old girl to become a polyandrous wife makes no sense – especially given that Mary Elizabeth Rollins Lightner would marry Adam Lightner in 1835. If she was told in 1831 that she was commanded by God to become a polygamous wife of Joseph Smith and did not appear to have any reservations about that declaration, then why would she marry another guy? As we will see with Fanny Alger, a lot of these late additions to the story don’t make a lot of sense.

Joseph Smith’s Relationship with Fanny Alger

As stated above, a key reason that the church wants to push the polygamy revelation back to 1831 is because just a few years later Joseph Smith will be caught in a scandalous relationship with Fanny Alger. Although no precise records exist, it is believed that around 1833 the then 27 year old Joseph Smith would engage in a sexual relationship with 16 year old Fanny Alger, who was a live-in maid at the Smith’s household.

The church’s essay contends the following:

“Fragmentary evidence suggests that Joseph Smith acted on the angel’s first command by marrying a plural wife, Fanny Alger, in Kirtland, Ohio, in the mid-1830s. Several Latter-day Saints who had lived in Kirtland reported decades later that Joseph Smith had married Alger, who lived and worked in the Smith household, after he had obtained her consent and that of her parents.”

First, there is no record of the marriage. This is important to note, because the account that the church cites to declare that this was a marriage is the following from Mosiah Hancock, a second hand source to his father Levi:

“Therefore Brother Joseph said ‘Brother Levi I want to make a bargain with you – If you will get Fanny Alger for me for a wife you may have Clarissa Reed. I love Fanny.' ‘I will' Said Father. ‘Go brother Levi and the Lord will prosper you' Said Joseph… Father goes to Fanny and said ‘Fanny Brother Joseph the Prophet loves you and wishes you for a wife will you be his wife?' ‘I will Levi' Said She – Father takes Fanny to Joseph and said ‘Brother Joseph I have been successful in my mission' – Father gave her to Joseph repeating the Ceremony as Joseph repeated to him.” (Mosiah Hancock Autobiography, pp. 62-63)

I want to state up front, as reflected in Mary Elizabeth Rollins Lightner’s quote above, that I do not believe Joseph Smith and Fanny Alger were married. There is no contemporary evidence, and the only account is a secondhand quote made 36 years after the relationship occurred. Furthermore, this quote dates to 1869—after polygamy was well established in the church—when there was significant motivation to link the Fanny Alger relationship to early polygamy, both to support Joseph Smith’s reputation and to authenticate the practice of polygamy. Following the split with the RLDS, the Brighamite branch (today’s LDS Church) needed to establish Joseph as a polygamist to assert their authority as his true successors.

If the church chooses to use this source to argue for a marriage between Joseph and Fanny, they must also explain Joseph’s own words about Fanny, used here as a 'bargaining chip' for Levi Hancock’s marriage. It may sound harsh, but consider this line attributed to Joseph Smith: “Brother Levi I want to make a bargain with you – If you will get Fanny Alger for me for a wife you may have Clarissa Reed. I love Fanny.”

To accept this account, one would have to believe that 27-year-old Joseph Smith fell in love with his 16-year-old live-in maid—contradicting the idea that any potential marriage was solely commanded by God. Joseph then apparently leveraged Levi Hancock’s love for Clarissa Reed as a “bargain” to persuade Levi to help him pursue Fanny. Even if one considers this a marriage, the details are troubling.

The Fanny Alger relationship created significant conflict for Joseph, including Emma Smith’s decision to expel Fanny from their home. Oliver Cowdery described it as follows:

“A dirty, nasty, filthy affair of his and Fanny Alger's was talked over in which I strictly declared that I had never deserted from the truth in the matter, and as I supposed was admitted by himself.” (Bushman, Rough Stone Rolling, p. 323)

This extramarital relationship played a major role in Oliver Cowdery’s eventual excommunication. It also led to Emma forcing Fanny out of the household.

The church’s essay states: “After the marriage with Alger ended in separation, Joseph seems to have set the subject of plural marriage aside until after the Church moved to Nauvoo, Illinois.”

However, the essay omits that the relationship (without any documented marriage despite the church’s claim) “ended in separation” because Emma expelled Fanny from the house. William McLellin, an early church member often considered a controversial source, told Joseph Smith’s son, Joseph Smith III:

“Again I told her [Emma] I heard that one night she missed Joseph and Fanny Alger. She went to the barn and saw him and Fanny in the barn together alone. She looked through a crack and saw the transaction!! She told me this story too was verily true.” (Compton, In Sacred Loneliness, p. 35)

After Emma discovered the nature of the relationship, she dismissed Fanny from their home. Ann Eliza Webb Young recounts:

Emma “had turned Fanny out of the house in the night…By degrees it became whispered about that Joseph’s love for his adopted daughter was by no means a paternal affection, and his wife, discovering the fact, at once took measures to place the girl beyond his reach. Angered at finding the two persons whom most she loved playing such a treacherous part towards her, she by no means spared her reproaches, and, finally, the storm became so furious, that Joseph was obliged to send, at midnight, for Oliver Cowdery, his scribe, to come and endeavor to settle matters between them.” (Young, Wife Number 19: Or The Story of a Life in Bondage, Being a Complete Exposé of Mormonism, And Revealing the Sorrows, Sacrifices and Sufferings of Women in Polygamy, p. 66)

Though Ann Eliza Webb Young later became critical of the church after her marriage to Brigham Young, she had direct knowledge of this event, as Fanny was sent back to her parents following her expulsion. Ann’s father, Chauncey Webb, also commented on the incident:

“Emma was furious, and drove the girl, who was unable to conceal the consequences of her celestial relation with the prophet, out of her house.” (Wyl, Wilhelm. “Mormon portraits: or the Truth About the Mormon Leaders, 1830-1866” p. 57)

In many of Joseph Smith’s later polygamous and polyandrous marriages, he engaged in relationships unknown to Emma, who actively opposed polygamy in her work with the Relief Society. Yet some of her closest friends were secretly married to Joseph. This dynamic may be uncomfortable, but it reflects historical facts we need to address.

Lastly, whether or not this was a marriage, it was not a “legal” relationship. Any marriage to Fanny Alger would have been illegal by law, and even more crucial for the church, because Joseph had not claimed the sealing keys at this point, it would not be a valid marriage in the eyes of God. As Oliver Cowdery, who would know if Joseph had sealing authority, believed, if it was not a marriage, it was simply an affair. Whichever perspective one takes, it occurred behind Emma’s back.

Fanny Alger’s case is critical because the relationship, whether marriage or affair, predates Joseph Smith’s claim to sealing keys. Beyond the technical illegality of any marriage, Joseph’s lack of authority to perform sealings at this time makes this relationship highly problematic. We will examine further relationships as we continue.

The 1835 Statement on Polygamy in the Doctrine and Covenants

Following the Fanny Alger relationship, Joseph Smith went on a trip to Michigan. When he was away, the church wrote the following ‘article on marriage’ which would be added to the Doctrine and Covenants in 1835:

“Inasmuch as this church of Christ has been reproached with the crime of fornication, and polygamy: we declare that we believe, that one man should have one wife; and one woman, but one husband, except in case of death, when either is at liberty to marry again.”

This statement wouldn’t be included in the Doctrine and Covenants unless rumors about Joseph Smith’s relationships outside his marriage to Emma were widespread. Todd Compton, a faithful member and author of In Sacred Loneliness, remarked that:

“clearly the statement represented an effort to counteract scandal and perhaps to defuse rumors of Fanny Alger’s marriage, possible pregnancy, and expulsion.” (Compton, In Sacred Loneliness, p. 36)

Although there is no direct evidence that Fanny Alger was pregnant when she was asked to leave the Smith home, this notion aligns with Chauncey Webb's account, which states that Fanny Alger “was unable to conceal the consequences of her celestial relation with the prophet” (Wyl, Wilhelm, Mormon Portraits: or the Truth About the Mormon Leaders, 1830-1866, p. 57). Some apologists point out that Joseph Smith didn’t personally write the article on marriage, which is accurate. However, he did insert the statement into the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants and later oversaw its publication in Times and Seasons when polygamy was gaining momentum. Thus, even if he wasn’t the original author, Joseph Smith’s involvement with the statement is undeniable.

The 1836 Vision and the Restoration of the Priesthood Keys

As previously mentioned, when Joseph Smith claimed in 1831 that leaders should marry Native Americans to make their posterity “white, just, and delightsome,” he had not yet introduced the concept of sealing keys. The same holds true for his relationship with Fanny Alger.

This brings us to the 1836 vision in the Kirtland Temple, where Joseph Smith claimed to receive the priesthood keys—later interpreted to include sealing keys. Known now as D&C 110, this vision was a shared experience between Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery.

As I noted in the priesthood restoration overview, it’s important to recognize that Joseph Smith reported visits from both Elias and Elijah during this vision, though they are the same individual with different names in translation. This raises questions about the vision itself, though it’s not the main issue regarding polygamy.

The church generally links this vision to the restoration of the sealing keys, yet a reading of D&C 110 shows no mention of sealings, eternal marriage, polygamy, or celestial marriage. The concept of “keys” appears here in a general sense, later adapted by the church to fit Joseph Smith’s evolving theology. The church’s essay on polygamy explains:

“The sealing of husband and wife for eternity was made possible by the restoration of priesthood keys and ordinances. On April 3, 1836, the Old Testament prophet Elijah appeared to Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery in the Kirtland Temple and restored the priesthood keys necessary to perform ordinances for the living and the dead, including sealing families together.”

There is no way Fanny Alger’s relationship could have constituted a marriage with proper sealing keys, nor could Joseph Smith’s 1831 revelation be connected to the Kirtland vision, as the concept of sealing had yet to be developed in Smith’s theology.

Joseph Smith’s 1841 Teaching on No Sin if No Accuser

As Joseph Smith was preparing to practice polygamy in late 1841, he introduced a teaching that he recorded in his journal. On November 7, 1841, he stated:

“I charged the Saints not to follow the example of the adversary in accusing the brethren, and said “if you do not accuse each other God will not accuse you. If you have no accuser you will enter heaven; and if you will follow the Revelations and instructions which God gives you through me, I will take you into heaven as my back load. If you will not accuse me, I will not accuse you. If you will throw a cloak of charity over my sins, I will over yours— for charity covereth a multitude of sins. What many people call sin is not sin; I do many things to break down superstition, and I will break it down:” (History, 1838–1856, volume C-1 Addenda)

This teaching is particularly intriguing in light of the rumors that had followed Joseph Smith since his relationship with Fanny Alger, which had sparked accusations of infidelity. Now, with the practice of polygamy beginning, these rumors were only set to grow.

Here, Joseph explicitly states that if members of the church do not accuse him of sins, he will not accuse them of theirs. Effectively, he reframes sin, encouraging members to “throw a cloak of charity” over his actions. This teaching bypasses traditional notions of accountability, suggesting that a lack of accusation alone can lead one to heaven—a notion that could discourage individuals from speaking out and instead binds them in a sort of mutual silence.

Joseph further asserts his authority by saying, “if you will follow the Revelations and instructions which God gives you through me, I will take you into heaven as my back load,” This reinforces that his words carry divine authority, a concept we see again as he makes proposals to various women. This notion will be significant as we examine Joseph Smith’s “Happiness Letter” to Nancy Rigdon, where similar ideas were used to pressure young women into marriage. These tactics are not unique to Joseph Smith; other leaders, including David Berg, David Koresh, and Warren Jeffs, have also claimed divine approval for their actions. Joseph’s emphasis on secrecy and his position as a prophet allowed him to leverage his authority and discourage accusations of adultery, as had been voiced by Oliver Cowdery regarding his relationship with Fanny Alger.

Joseph Smith’s Proposals to Polygamous and Polyandrous Wives

It’s challenging to present the history of polygamy chronologically, but before we discuss D&C 132, it’s helpful to consider how Joseph Smith practiced polygamy before the revelation was recorded.

One significant aspect of Joseph’s practice involved polyandry—marrying women who were already legally married to other men. The church acknowledges this in its essay on plural marriage:

“Following his marriage to Louisa Beaman and before he married other single women, Joseph Smith was sealed to a number of women who were already married.”

While the essay doesn’t provide details, historian Todd Compton estimates that eleven of Joseph Smith’s wives were polyandrous, with nine out of his first twelve marriages involving already-married women (Compton, In Sacred Loneliness). In the essay, the church explains:

“There are several possible explanations for this practice. These sealings may have provided a way to create an eternal bond or link between Joseph’s family and other families within the Church… Joseph Smith’s sealings to women already married may have been an early version of linking one family to another. In Nauvoo, most if not all of the first husbands seem to have continued living in the same household with their wives during Joseph’s lifetime, and complaints about these sealings with Joseph Smith are virtually absent from the documentary record.”

The church offers some explanations, suggesting that these sealings created “an eternal bond or link” between Joseph’s family and others in the church. However, if this were the only purpose, Joseph could have sealed himself to one woman per family or performed non-marital sealings with men. Additionally, the Law of Adoption, which Joseph occasionally practiced, allowed men to be sealed as adopted sons. So why choose wives and young women, instead of allowing these women to marry partners closer to their own age?

This argument weakens further when we consider Joseph’s marriages to multiple women within the same family, including pairs of sisters and mothers and daughters. Such arrangements suggest purposes beyond simple kinship bonding, especially considering that some of these relationships were shrouded in secrecy and involved the need to “burn letters” after reading.

If the sole purpose of these marriages were indeed noble and virtuous, transparency would have been possible. Joseph could have explained the practice openly, avoiding secrecy and lying. Legal non-romantic sealings between families would not have required deception, nor would they have caused such distress to families and young women.

The Production of Doctrine and Covenants 132

There is so much history the occurs between Fanny Alger and when D&C 132 was produced, but I want to quickly cover D&C 132 here because I feel it is important to understanding how Joseph Smith produced revelation. This revelation was first written down on July 12, 1843, and was dictated to William Clayton as the request of Joseph’s brother, Hyrum Smith.

Hyrum Smith wanted Joseph to produce this revelation as Hyrum had been convinced of the idea of polygamy, and believed that he could convince Joseph’s first wife Emma of the principal if it was written in the voice of God. The church has the narrative of this event in their book Saints:

"On the morning of July 12, William Clayton was in Joseph’s office when the prophet and Hyrum entered. “If you will write the revelation,” Hyrum told Joseph, “I will take and read it to Emma, and I believe I can convince her of its truth, and you will hereafter have peace.”

“You do not know Emma as well as I do,” Joseph said. That spring and summer, he had been sealed to additional women, including a few whom Emma had personally selected. Yet helping Joseph choose wives had not made obeying the principle easy for Emma.

“The doctrine is so plain,” Hyrum said. “I can convince any reasonable man or woman of its truth, purity, and heavenly origin.”

“We will see,” Joseph said. He asked William to take out paper and write as he spoke the word of the Lord.

Much of the revelation was already known to Joseph. It described the new and everlasting covenant of eternal marriage, along with associated blessings and promises. It also revealed the terms governing plural marriage, which Joseph had learned while translating the Bible in 1831. The remainder of the revelation was new counsel for him and Emma, addressing their questions and current struggles with plural marriage." (Saints, Volume 1)

As noted earlier, Joseph Smith’s statements in 1831 significantly differ from events in Nauvoo. In 1831, he spoke about marrying Lamanite women to make their posterity “white, delightsome, and just,” language derived from the Book of Mormon, which claims that Native Americans could be made "white" by conversion to Christ. This teaching, now understood as scientifically flawed and racist, is why neither Saints nor the official essay explain that 1831 revelation, as it relies on outdated beliefs from the Book of Mormon and Smith’s early revelations.

Another critical omission from Saints is that, by this time, Joseph Smith had already married about 20 other women, with Emma unaware of most. Saints mentions “including a few whom Emma had personally selected,” but omits that Emma chose four women for Smith to marry, two of whom, sisters Emily and Eliza Partridge, he had already married.

Emily and Eliza Partridge lived in the Smith household after their father Edward’s death in May 1840. Joseph married them in March 1843, two months before Emma selected them as wives for him, leading him to conduct a "mock wedding" with the sisters to avoid disclosing to Emma that he was already married to, and intimate with, these women living in their home.

We’ll explore these marriages in greater detail, but it’s essential to understand that D&C 132 arose after years of Smith deceiving Emma, maintaining secret relationships with other women, many of whom were her close friends. I can’t even imagine what that must have felt like for Emma as she began to realize what Joseph Smith was doing, and this is why polygamy makes me so upset.

Hyrum Smith’s request for Joseph to dictate what is now known as D&C 132 is particularly interesting in light of earlier revelations, including those in the Book of Mormon. William Clayton, who recorded the revelation, noted:

“Hyrum very urgently requested Joseph to write the revelation by means of the Urim and Thummim, but Joseph, in reply, said he did not need to, for he knew the revelation perfectly from beginning to end.” (History of the Church)

Hyrum requested that Joseph use the Urim and Thummim (or seer stone) to ensure the revelation was directly from God. Instead, Joseph claimed he didn’t need it because he knew the revelation by heart. D&C 132 is over 3,200 words long, so it’s improbable that anyone could memorize it verbatim. By Joseph’s account, this revelation had been given years earlier but not documented.

As mentioned in the Book of Mormon authorship overview, a typical scribe could write about 1,200 words per hour, meaning that dictating D&C 132 would take the equivalent of a full day’s work—yet Joseph did this without any source material or a seer stone.

The revelation is written in a style reminiscent of the King James Bible, mirroring other revelations with phrases like “I am the Lord thy God” and “Behold, I am Alpha and Omega.” Comparing this revelation to Joseph’s initial 1832 First Vision account reveals his skill in switching from everyday speech to a divine “voice of God,” even while dictating.

Given that Hyrum requested the revelation to convince Emma, the text’s tone and emphasis make sense. Much of the revelation justifies Joseph’s actions and threatens to “destroy” Emma if she does not comply. While apologists might argue Joseph received these instructions years prior, the absence of any earlier record, despite his documenting numerous other revelations, lends credence to critics’ arguments that he invoked the “voice of God” to assert authority or validate his actions.

Notably, D&C 132 appears to have been given before Joseph entered polygamy, yet the portion directed at Emma reads as if it responds to her recent awareness of his additional wives. From D&C 132:

Verily, I say unto you: A commandment I give unto mine handmaid, Emma Smith, your wife, whom I have given unto you, that she stay herself and partake not of that which I commanded you to offer unto her; for I did it, saith the Lord, to prove you all, as I did Abraham, and that I might require an offering at your hand, by covenant and sacrifice. (Doctrine and Covenants. Section 132:51)

What Joseph “offered” Emma remains unclear. Critics refer to a journal entry by William Clayton, written shortly before D&C 132’s dictation, which states:

"This A.M. President Joseph took me and conversed considerable concerning some delicate matters. Said [Emma] wanted to lay a snare for me. He told me last night of this and said he had felt troubled. He said [Emma] had treated him coldly and badly since I came…and he knew she was disposed to be revenged on him for some things. She thought that if he would indulge himself she would too." (William Clayton Journal, June 23, 1843)

Apologists suggest Joseph might have offered Emma a divorce, as FAIR notes:

"Some have seen this as Emma claiming she would practice plural marriage (a strange idea, given how she felt about it), and these readers have then extended the reading to include a belief that she was threatening to marry William Law. Others have seen these verses (perhaps more plausibly) as Emma simply threatening divorce if Joseph didn't cease plural marriage."

However, critics argue that Emma proposed taking another husband if Joseph had multiple wives, citing Clayton’s record: “She thought that if he would indulge himself she would too.” D&C 132 frames the offer as an “Abrahamic test” for Emma to “partake not of,” which seems inconsistent with a divorce proposal.

Joseph claimed to know this revelation perfectly, though it references what seems like a recent offer to Emma. The fact that he did not use the Urim and Thummim raises questions about how he knew to include this clause. This passage suggests he tailored the revelation for Emma as he dictated it, knowing Hyrum would bring it to her.

This might also explain the revelation’s harsh language, which includes threats of destruction if Emma did not comply: “But if she will not abide this commandment she shall be destroyed, saith the Lord; for I am the Lord thy God, and will destroy her if she abide not in my law.”

Apologists might view this as a generalized command, unrelated to polygamy, yet it’s striking that God would demand Emma forgive Joseph “his trespasses” in a revelation supposedly given before he took additional wives: "And again, verily I say, let mine handmaid forgive my servant Joseph his trespasses; and then shall she be forgiven her trespasses, wherein she has trespassed against me."

Joseph’s revelations frequently address his immediate issues, such as pressuring Martin Harris to sell property, ordering the destruction of Hiram Page’s seer stone, or explaining why he could not recreate the first 116 pages by divine command.

Given Joseph’s willingness to dictate in a scriptural voice, these “revelations” often appear to serve his personal needs. If one accepts that D&C 132 was self-authored, it explains why the justification for polygamy feels flawed. In D&C 132, God allegedly tells Joseph why He “ justified my servants Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, as also Moses, David and Solomon, my servants, as touching the principle and doctrine of their having many wives and concubines"

However, Abraham was never commanded to take a second wife, and the Book of Mormon condemns David and Solomon’s polygamy as “abominable.” Such contradictions trouble critics because it seems implausible that God would so directly contradict earlier scriptures. Yet, considering D&C 132 in light of Clayton’s journal entries during the dictation offers insight into the broader complexities within Joseph Smith’s work.

The reality is that pretty much all of the warnings were direct at Emma. In fact, D&C 132 has 11 warnings included in it. I’m including them below with super quick commentary on each:

4 For behold, I reveal unto you a new and an everlasting covenant; and if ye abide not that covenant, then are ye damned; for no one can reject this covenant and be permitted to enter into my glory.

This is a threat directed at Emma in just the fourth verse, that if she does not abide by the revelation Joseph Smith is dictating, she will be damned.

6 And as pertaining to the new and everlasting covenant, it was instituted for the fulness of my glory; and he that receiveth a fulness thereof must and shall abide the law, or he shall be damned, saith the Lord God.

This threat is to Joseph Smith, but it really is again targeted at Emma. Basically this is stating that if Joseph Smith does not follow through with polygamy, he will be damned. This is a threat directly at Emma Smith that she should “partake not of that which I commanded you to offer unto her.” There is speculation as to what that offer was as noted above, with some accounts that it was an offer to let Emma Smith have another man, and some apologists contend it might have been an offer for divorce. This is a threat that is not pointed at Emma or indirectly at her through Joseph, but one that is stating that any of Joseph Smith’s polygamous or polyandrous wives that claimed to be pure but are not pure will be destroyed. This is the most famous of the direct threats to Emma Smith, with Joseph Smith telling his wife in the voice of God that she will be destroyed if she does not abide in the new and everlasting covenant, which was without question considered to be polygamy at this time.

You cannot reconcile this – in the Book of Mormon God says they are committing whoredoms and that the practice is abominable, and then in D&C 132 he says “in nothing did they sin” and that those wives and concubines where “given unto him of me… and in none of these things did he sin against me…”

Holding Marriage Contracts Valid and Fulfilled

In the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants, we discussed above the section that rejects polygamy, but there’s another line that is completely destroyed by D&C 132. From the original 1835 Doctrine and Covenants:

“All legal contracts of marriage made before a person is baptized into this church, should be held sacred and fulfilled.” (The Joseph Smith Papers. Doctrine and Covenants, 1835, Section 101)

Compare that to D&C 132:

7 “All covenants, contracts, bonds, obligations, oaths, vows, performances, connections, associations, or expectations, that are not made and entered into and sealed by the Holy Spirit of promise… are of no efficacy, virtue, or force in and after the resurrection from the dead; for all contracts that are not made unto this end have an end when men are dead.”

To be clear, the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants states that the church will hold all legal contracts of marriage as sacred and fulfilled, but D&C 132 says those same legal contracts “end when men are dead.”

While apologists could contend that marriages have the “til death do us part” clause, this is simply not how any Christian religion believes their families will be after death. This is an area where Joseph Smith is creating a problem to sell you the solution, but it absolutely contradicts the spirit of holding “legal contracts of marriage… sacred and fulfilled.”

The Law of Sarah

We are going to cover many of the problems with D&C 132 in our third polygamy overview as we look at some of the ways Joseph Smith proposed to other women, but I want to cover how ridiculous the “Law of Sarah” is very quickly. From the church’s essay:

“The revelation on marriage required that a wife give her consent before her husband could enter into plural marriage.4 Nevertheless, toward the end of the revelation, the Lord said that if the first wife “receive not this law”—the command to practice plural marriage—the husband would be “exempt from the law of Sarah,” presumably the requirement that the husband gain the consent of the first wife before marrying additional women. After Emma opposed plural marriage (and before, since Joseph never asked early on and just married women behind her back), Joseph was placed in an agonizing dilemma, forced to choose between the will of God and the will of his beloved Emma. This really sums of the Law of Sarah nicely.
